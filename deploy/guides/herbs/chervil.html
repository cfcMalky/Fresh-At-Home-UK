Grow Your Own Chervil

Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Chervil in the UK

Getting Started

Chervil (Anthriscus cerefolium) is a delicate, aromatic herb with a mild aniseed flavour that's perfect for adding subtle flavour to salads, soups, and egg dishes. This cool-season annual herb is easy to grow and thrives in the UK's climate, making it an excellent choice for both beginner and experienced gardeners. Chervil is a key ingredient in French fines herbes and adds a fresh, light flavour to many dishes.

Key Characteristics:

  • Cool-season annual herb
  • Fast-growing and easy to cultivate
  • Prefers partial shade and cool conditions
  • Can be grown in containers or in the ground
  • Self-seeds readily for continuous harvests
  • Delicate, fern-like leaves with mild aniseed flavour

Chervil is a member of the carrot family and has delicate, lacy leaves that resemble parsley but with a more subtle, aniseed-like flavour. It's a cool-season herb that grows best in spring and autumn, often bolting (going to seed) in hot summer weather. This makes it perfect for UK gardens, where it can be grown almost year-round with successive sowings.

Unlike many herbs that prefer full sun, chervil actually prefers partial shade, especially in warmer weather. This makes it ideal for growing in shadier spots in the garden or on a windowsill. The delicate leaves are best used fresh, as they lose much of their flavour when dried.

Choosing Varieties

While there are a few varieties of chervil available, the common chervil (Anthriscus cerefolium) is the most widely grown and suitable for UK gardens. There's also curly chervil, which has more tightly curled leaves, though it's less commonly available.

Common chervil is the standard variety used in cooking and has the characteristic delicate, fern-like leaves. All varieties have similar growing requirements and can be used interchangeably in recipes.

Recommended Varieties

Common Chervil

The standard variety with delicate, fern-like leaves. Most widely available and reliable for UK growing conditions.

Curly Chervil

Features more tightly curled leaves. Less common but offers attractive ornamental value alongside culinary use.

Sowing Chervil

Sowing Indoors

For an early start, sow chervil seeds indoors from late winter to early spring. Chervil doesn't transplant well, so sow directly into individual pots or modules to avoid disturbing the roots.

  1. Prepare containers: Fill small pots or modules with peat-free seed compost. Chervil prefers not to be transplanted, so individual containers work best.
  2. Sow seeds: Sow seeds thinly, about 0.5cm (¬ºin) deep. Cover lightly with compost and water gently.
  3. Provide cool conditions: Chervil germinates best at 15‚Äì18¬∞C (59‚Äì64¬∞F). Keep in a cool, bright location away from direct sunlight.
  4. Water carefully: Keep compost moist but not waterlogged. Chervil seedlings are delicate and prone to damping off if overwatered.
  5. Harden off: Before planting out, gradually acclimatise plants to outdoor conditions over 7‚Äì10 days.

Sowing Outdoors

Chervil can be sown directly outdoors from early spring to late summer. For best results, sow in succession every 2‚Äì3 weeks to ensure continuous harvests. Choose a partially shaded spot with well-drained soil.

Sow seeds thinly in drills about 1cm (¬Ωin) deep, spacing rows 20‚Äì30cm (8‚Äì12in) apart. Thin seedlings to 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart when they're large enough to handle. Chervil can also be broadcast sown in a patch and thinned as needed.

üí° Succession Sowing: Chervil bolts quickly in hot weather. To maintain a continuous supply, sow small batches every 2‚Äì3 weeks from early spring through to late summer. In very hot weather, skip a sowing or provide extra shade.

Planting

Chervil can be grown in containers, raised beds, or directly in the ground. It prefers partial shade, especially in warmer weather, making it ideal for shadier spots in the garden.

Site Selection

Choose a partially shaded location with well-drained, fertile soil. Chervil prefers cool conditions and will bolt quickly in full sun during hot weather. A spot that receives morning sun and afternoon shade is ideal, or a position that gets dappled shade throughout the day.

Container Planting

Chervil grows well in containers, making it perfect for patios, balconies, and windowsills. Use pots at least 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) deep with good drainage. Fill with peat-free multi-purpose compost and plant seedlings 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart. Container-grown chervil can be moved to shadier positions during hot weather to prevent bolting.

Ground Planting

If planting in the ground, prepare the soil by removing weeds and incorporating well-rotted compost or organic matter. Plant seedlings at the same depth they were growing in their pots, spacing them 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart. Water well after planting and keep the soil moist until plants are established.

üí° Companion Planting: Chervil is said to improve the flavour of nearby vegetables and can help deter slugs. It's a good companion for radishes, lettuce, and other leafy greens.

Plant Care

Chervil is relatively low-maintenance but benefits from regular watering and attention to prevent bolting. Keep plants well-watered and provide shade during hot weather to extend the harvest period.

Watering

Keep chervil plants well-watered, especially during dry spells. The soil should be kept consistently moist but not waterlogged. Container-grown plants will need more frequent watering, especially in warm weather. Water at the base of plants to avoid wetting the delicate leaves, which can lead to fungal problems.

Feeding

Chervil doesn't usually need additional feeding if grown in reasonably fertile soil. However, container-grown plants may benefit from a light liquid feed every 4‚Äì6 weeks during the growing season. Use a balanced, organic fertiliser at half strength.

Preventing Bolting

Chervil has a tendency to bolt (go to seed) in hot weather or when stressed. To delay bolting, keep plants well-watered, provide shade during the hottest part of the day, and harvest regularly. Once plants start to bolt, the leaves become less flavourful, so it's best to remove bolting plants and sow a fresh batch.

Harvesting to Encourage Growth

Regular harvesting encourages new growth and helps delay bolting. Pick leaves from the outside of the plant, leaving the central growing point intact. This encourages the plant to produce more leaves and extends the harvest period.

Self-Seeding

Chervil self-seeds readily, so if you allow some plants to flower and set seed, you'll often get volunteer seedlings the following year. These can be left to grow or transplanted to desired locations. Self-seeded plants often appear at the perfect time for your local conditions.

Harvesting

Chervil leaves can be harvested as soon as plants are large enough, usually 6‚Äì8 weeks after sowing. The leaves are best used fresh, as they lose much of their delicate flavour when dried.

How to Harvest

  • Pick young leaves: Harvest leaves when they're young and tender for the best flavour. Older leaves can become tough and less flavourful.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick leaves from the outside of the plant regularly to encourage new growth and delay bolting.
  • Cut whole stems: You can cut whole stems near the base, or pick individual leaves as needed. Regular harvesting encourages bushier growth.
  • Use immediately: Chervil is best used fresh. The delicate flavour diminishes quickly after picking, so use within a few hours if possible.

Using Fresh Chervil

Chervil is a key ingredient in French fines herbes (along with parsley, chives, and tarragon) and adds a delicate, aniseed-like flavour to many dishes. It's excellent in salads, egg dishes, soups, and sauces. Add chervil at the end of cooking or use raw, as heat diminishes its flavour. It pairs particularly well with fish, chicken, and vegetables.

Storage

Fresh chervil can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days. Place stems in a glass of water like cut flowers, or wrap in damp kitchen paper and store in the fridge. Chervil doesn't dry well, losing most of its flavour, so it's best used fresh. If you must preserve it, freezing is better than drying, though fresh is always preferable.

Common Problems

Chervil is generally trouble-free, but there are a few issues to watch out for. Most problems are related to growing conditions, particularly heat stress causing premature bolting.

Pests

  • Slugs and snails: These can damage young seedlings and leaves. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls. Chervil's delicate leaves are particularly attractive to slugs.
  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Remove by hand or spray with a strong jet of water. Usually not a serious problem if plants are healthy.

Diseases

  • Damping off: Fungal disease that affects seedlings, causing them to collapse. Prevent by avoiding overwatering, ensuring good air circulation, and using clean compost.
  • Powdery mildew: Can occur in humid conditions or when plants are stressed. Improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering. Usually not serious if caught early.

Environmental Issues

  • Bolting: Chervil bolts quickly in hot weather or when stressed. Provide shade, keep well-watered, and harvest regularly to delay. Once bolting starts, remove plants and sow a fresh batch.
  • Poor germination: Chervil seeds can be slow to germinate, especially if conditions are too warm. Ensure cool conditions (15‚Äì18¬∞C) and keep compost moist but not waterlogged.
  • Leggy growth: Can occur if plants don't receive enough light or are overcrowded. Ensure adequate spacing and provide bright, indirect light.

üí° Growing Tip: Chervil's tendency to bolt in hot weather is actually a natural response. Rather than fighting it, work with it by making regular succession sowings. This ensures you always have fresh, tender leaves available.
