Grow Your Own Dill

Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Dill in the UK

Getting Started

Dill (Anethum graveolens) is an aromatic annual herb with feathery, fern-like leaves and distinctive yellow umbel flowers. This versatile herb is essential in many cuisines, particularly Scandinavian, Eastern European, and Middle Eastern dishes. Dill is easy to grow in UK gardens and provides both fresh leaves and seeds for culinary use.

Key Characteristics:

  • Fast-growing annual herb
  • Provides both fresh leaves and seeds
  • Easy to grow from seed
  • Can be grown in containers or in the ground
  • Self-seeds readily for continuous harvests
  • Attractive to beneficial insects when flowering

Dill is a member of the carrot family and has a distinctive aniseed-like flavour that's both fresh and aromatic. The feathery leaves are used fresh in cooking, while the seeds have a stronger, more concentrated flavour. Dill is particularly famous for its use in pickling, especially with cucumbers, but it's also excellent in salads, fish dishes, and many other recipes.

One of the benefits of growing dill is that it attracts beneficial insects, particularly when it flowers. The yellow umbel flowers are rich in nectar and attract hoverflies, which help control aphids in your garden. Dill also self-seeds readily, so once you've grown it once, you'll often get volunteer plants appearing in subsequent years.

Choosing Varieties

While there are a few varieties of dill available, the main differences are in plant height and leaf characteristics. Most varieties have similar flavour profiles, so choice often comes down to space available and whether you're growing primarily for leaves or seeds.

Standard dill varieties grow tall (up to 1m / 3ft) and are best for seed production. Dwarf or compact varieties are better for containers and leaf production. All varieties have the characteristic feathery leaves and aniseed flavour.

Recommended Varieties

Standard Dill

The classic variety that grows tall (up to 1m / 3ft). Excellent for seed production and traditional uses. Best for larger gardens.

'Bouquet'

Compact variety that's ideal for containers and smaller gardens. Good leaf production and earlier flowering than standard types.

'Fernleaf'

Dwarf variety with particularly fine, fern-like leaves. Perfect for containers and small spaces. Slower to bolt than standard varieties.

'Dukat'

Variety with excellent flavour and good leaf production. Slower to bolt, making it ideal for continuous leaf harvests.

Sowing Dill

Sowing Outdoors (Recommended)

Dill is best sown directly where it's to grow, as it has a long taproot and doesn't transplant well. Sow from early spring to early summer for continuous harvests throughout the season.

  1. Prepare the site: Choose a sunny location with well-drained soil. Remove weeds and rake the soil to a fine tilth.
  2. Sow seeds: Sow seeds thinly in drills about 1cm (¬Ωin) deep, spacing rows 30cm (12in) apart. Alternatively, broadcast sow in a prepared area.
  3. Water gently: Water the soil gently after sowing, taking care not to wash away the seeds. Keep the soil moist until germination.
  4. Thin seedlings: When seedlings are large enough to handle, thin them to 20‚Äì30cm (8‚Äì12in) apart. Use the thinnings in salads or cooking.
  5. Succession sow: For continuous harvests, sow small batches every 2‚Äì3 weeks from early spring to early summer.

Sowing Indoors

If you must start dill indoors, use deep pots (at least 10cm / 4in deep) to accommodate the long taproot. Sow seeds about 1cm (¬Ωin) deep and keep in a warm location (15‚Äì20¬∞C / 59‚Äì68¬∞F). Transplant carefully when seedlings are large enough, taking care not to disturb the roots.

Planting

Dill prefers a sunny position with well-drained soil. It can be grown in containers, raised beds, or directly in the ground. The key is to avoid disturbing the roots, so direct sowing is preferred over transplanting.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny location with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. Dill prefers full sun but will tolerate light shade. Avoid windy positions, as tall varieties can be damaged by strong winds. Dill grows best in soil that's not too rich, as overly fertile soil can encourage excessive leafy growth at the expense of flavour.

Container Planting

Dill can be grown in containers, but choose deep pots (at least 30cm / 12in deep) to accommodate the long taproot. Use peat-free multi-purpose compost and sow seeds directly in the container. Thin seedlings to 20‚Äì30cm (8‚Äì12in) apart. Compact varieties like 'Fernleaf' are ideal for container growing.

Ground Planting

If planting in the ground, prepare the soil by removing weeds and incorporating well-rotted compost if the soil is very poor. However, avoid over-enriching the soil. Sow seeds directly where plants are to grow, as dill doesn't transplant well. Space plants 20‚Äì30cm (8‚Äì12in) apart for standard varieties, or closer for compact types.

üí° Companion Planting: Dill is an excellent companion plant. It attracts beneficial insects like hoverflies, which help control aphids. It's particularly good near brassicas, lettuce, and onions. However, avoid planting near fennel as they can cross-pollinate.

Plant Care

Dill is relatively low-maintenance once established. Regular watering, occasional feeding, and supporting tall varieties will keep plants healthy and productive.

Watering

Water dill regularly during dry spells, especially when plants are young. Once established, dill is quite drought-tolerant, but regular watering will encourage more vigorous growth and better harvests. Container-grown plants will need more frequent watering than those in the ground. Water at the base of plants to avoid wetting the leaves.

Feeding

Dill doesn't usually need additional feeding if grown in reasonably fertile soil. In fact, overly rich soil can encourage excessive leafy growth at the expense of flavour. However, container-grown plants may benefit from a light liquid feed every 4‚Äì6 weeks during the growing season. Use a balanced, organic fertiliser at half strength.

Supporting

Tall varieties of dill may need support, especially in windy positions. Use stakes or plant supports to prevent plants from falling over. This is particularly important when plants are flowering and setting seed, as the seed heads can be heavy.

Harvesting to Encourage Growth

Regular harvesting of leaves encourages new growth and helps delay flowering. Pick leaves from the outside of the plant, leaving the central growing point intact. However, if you want to harvest seeds, allow some plants to flower and set seed.

Self-Seeding

Dill self-seeds readily, so if you allow some plants to flower and set seed, you'll often get volunteer seedlings the following year. These can be left to grow or transplanted to desired locations (though transplanting is not ideal). Self-seeded plants often appear at the perfect time for your local conditions.

Harvesting

Dill leaves can be harvested as soon as plants are large enough, usually 6‚Äì8 weeks after sowing. The leaves are best used fresh, as they lose much of their flavour when dried. Seeds can be harvested when they turn brown and dry on the plant.

How to Harvest Leaves

  • Pick young leaves: Harvest leaves when they're young and tender for the best flavour. Older leaves can become tough and less flavourful.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick leaves from the outside of the plant regularly to encourage new growth and delay flowering.
  • Cut whole stems: You can cut whole stems near the base, or pick individual leaves as needed. Regular harvesting encourages bushier growth.
  • Use immediately: Dill leaves are best used fresh. The flavour diminishes quickly after picking, so use within a few hours if possible.

Harvesting Seeds

To harvest dill seeds, allow plants to flower and set seed. When the seed heads turn brown and dry, cut the stems and hang them upside down in a paper bag in a warm, dry place. The seeds will fall into the bag as they dry. Alternatively, place seed heads in a paper bag and shake to release the seeds. Store dried seeds in an airtight container.

Using Your Dill

Dill leaves are essential in many cuisines, particularly Scandinavian, Eastern European, and Middle Eastern dishes. They add a fresh, aniseed-like flavour to fish dishes, salads, soups, and many other recipes. Dill is particularly famous for its use in pickling, especially with cucumbers. Add dill leaves at the end of cooking or use raw, as heat diminishes their flavour. The seeds have a stronger, more concentrated flavour and are used in spice blends, pickling, and baking.

Storage

Fresh dill leaves can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days. Place stems in a glass of water like cut flowers, or wrap in damp kitchen paper and store in the fridge. Dill leaves don't dry well, losing most of their flavour, so they're best used fresh. However, they can be frozen for use in cooked dishes. The seeds store well in an airtight container for up to a year.

Common Problems

Dill is generally trouble-free, but there are a few issues to watch out for. Most problems are minor and easily managed.

Pests

  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. However, dill's flowers attract hoverflies, which are natural predators of aphids. Usually not a serious problem if plants are healthy.
  • Slugs and snails: Can damage young seedlings. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls. Once established, dill is less attractive to slugs.
  • Carrot fly: Can affect dill as it's related to carrots. Use barriers or companion planting to deter them.

Diseases

  • Powdery mildew: Can occur in humid conditions or when plants are overcrowded. Improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering. Usually not serious if caught early.
  • Damping off: Fungal disease that affects seedlings. Prevent by avoiding overwatering, ensuring good air circulation, and using clean compost.

Environmental Issues

  • Bolting: Dill will eventually bolt (go to seed) as it's an annual. This is natural and provides seeds for next year. To delay bolting, keep plants well-watered and harvest leaves regularly.
  • Wind damage: Tall varieties can be damaged by strong winds. Provide support or choose a sheltered position.
  • Poor growth: Can occur if plants don't receive enough water or are growing in very poor soil. Ensure adequate watering and improve soil with compost if needed.
