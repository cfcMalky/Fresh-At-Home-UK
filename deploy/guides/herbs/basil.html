Grow Your Own Basil

Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Basil in the UK

Getting Started

Basil is a popular tender herb that's easy to grow from seed and thrives in warmth and sunshine, providing abundant harvests of aromatic leaves throughout summer. This versatile herb is ideal for even the smallest garden, growing beautifully in containers, in the ground, or indoors on a bright windowsill.

Key Characteristics:

  • Easy to grow from seed
  • Thrives in warm, sunny conditions
  • Perfect for containers and small spaces
  • Can be grown indoors on windowsills
  • Harvest leaves throughout summer
  • Attracts beneficial pollinators when flowering

Basil is a useful culinary herb to grow at home, but it isn't hardy in the UK ‚Äì it needs warm conditions, so grows well in a greenhouse or on an indoor windowsill. It can be grown outside in summer, ideally in a sunny, sheltered spot. Many types of basil are perennial, but because they need a lot of warmth and sunlight, they are usually treated as annuals and discarded in autumn.

Basil makes an attractive addition to herb gardens and vegetable plots, and even flower borders and container displays, especially varieties with coloured or ruffled leaves. Plants often start flowering in mid- to late summer, which does affect the flavour of the leaves, but the nectar-rich flowers are enjoyed by bees.

üí° Quick Tip: Basil can also be grown as microgreens on a sunny windowsill. In just a couple of weeks you'll have tasty, colourful and nutritious seedlings to add to salads and sandwiches.

Choosing Varieties

There are many species and varieties of basil (Ocimum) to choose from, ranging from commonly used sweet basil to spicy, aniseed-flavoured Thai basil, as well as lemon, lime and cinnamon flavours. Many species of basil have medicinal as well as culinary uses, such as holy basil.

The aromatic leaves of basil may be ruffled or smooth, large or small, and various shades of green or purple. Most form fairly compact plants, 20‚Äì50cm (8‚Äì20in) tall and are kept bushy by regular picking.

Recommended Varieties

'Aroma 2'

A vigorous F1 hybrid with sweet, aromatic large green leaves. Good resistance to bolting and disease.

'Cinnamon'

Features distinctive cinnamon-flavoured leaves with purple stems and flowers. Adds unique flavour to dishes.

'Lemon'

Bright, citrusy flavour perfect for fish dishes, salads, and teas. Compact growth habit.

'Purple'

Eye-catching purple leaves that provide both visual appeal and flavour. Great for garnishes.

'Thai'

Spicy, aniseed-flavoured leaves essential for Asian cuisine. Tolerates heat well.

'Magic Mountain'

Attractive variety with good flower production, excellent for pollinators. Maintains leaf quality well.

Sowing Basil

Sowing Indoors

For the best results, sow basil seeds indoors from early spring onwards. Basil needs warmth to germinate, ideally 15‚Äì25¬∞C (59‚Äì77¬∞F), so use a heated propagator or place in a warm location. If sowing several small batches a few weeks apart, you'll have harvests over a longer period.

  1. Prepare your containers: Fill seed trays or small pots with peat-free seed compost.
  2. Sow the seeds: Sow seeds thinly on the surface, without covering them, as they need light to germinate.
  3. Provide warmth: Keep in a warm, bright location (15‚Äì25¬∞C / 59‚Äì77¬∞F) such as on a windowsill or in a heated propagator.
  4. Water carefully: Water regularly but avoid overwatering, as seedlings are prone to fungal diseases such as damping off.
  5. Pot on: When seedlings have several leaves, move them into their own 7.5cm (3in) pot of peat-free multi-purpose compost or plant several in a larger pot, 7.5cm (3in) apart.

Sowing Microgreens

You can also sow basil seeds more densely in trays for harvesting as microgreens. They will be ready to harvest within 10 to 14 days, depending on light levels and temperature. Sow at any time of year, keep on a warm bright windowsill and water regularly ‚Äì you'll soon have tasty, nutritious seedlings to snip and scatter in salads and other dishes.

Sowing Outdoors

Basil can be sown outdoors but results are likely to be less successful than indoor sowings. If sowing outdoors, wait until early summer, once night-time temperatures consistently stay above 10¬∞C. Sowing outdoors gives a shorter growing season and is only suitable for warm locations.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Growing in containers is often more successful than in the ground, as they provide the free-draining conditions basil needs, and plants are easier to protect from slugs and snails.

Planting

You can plant out newly bought basil plants and indoor-sown plants in early summer. Harden off the young plants first, to acclimatise them to outdoor conditions.

Site Selection

Basil needs a warm, sunny, sheltered location, and usually does best in a container, but can also be planted in well-drained soil.

Container Planting

Choose a container at least 20cm (8in) wide and deep, and fill with peat-free multi-purpose compost. Plant basil at the same level it was previously growing, and space plants at least 10cm (4in) apart.

üí° Greenhouse Growing: As basil loves warmth, it does particularly well in a greenhouse, in pots, growing bags or a greenhouse border. This is the best way to grow it in cooler locations.

Plant Care

Once established, basil is easy to look after ‚Äì just keep it warm, provide sufficient moisture and keep plants bushy and productive by harvesting the shoot tips regularly.

Watering

Water newly planted basil well until it's established, then water during dry spells to keep the soil or compost evenly moist. Plants in greenhouses and containers will need regular watering. When watering, try to avoid splashing the leaves and water in the morning if possible, as basil hates having wet roots overnight. Basil may start to flower sooner in dry conditions.

Feeding

When growing basil in the ground, there's usually no need for feeding, but plants in containers can run short of nutrients, so you could apply an organic-based, balanced liquid fertiliser to boost leafy growth. Avoid using potassium-rich fertilisers like tomato feed, as these encourage flowering.

Removing Flowers

Basil usually starts to flower in mid- to late summer ‚Äì you can delay this and maintain leaf quality for as long as possible by removing any flower stems as soon as you spot them. It's worth letting plants bloom eventually though, or allowing one to flower early if you have several plants, as basil flowers are rich in nectar, providing food for bees and other insects.

Propagating

Although basil is usually grown from seed, it's easy to grow new plants from softwood cuttings taken during summer. Snip off a non-flowering shoot just below a leaf, remove the lower leaves and pinch out the tip, then stand the cutting in a jar of water. It will quickly form roots and can be planted in a pot of peat-free multi-purpose compost after just a few weeks.

Harvesting

Basil leaves can be harvested throughout the summer. Pick leaves as required, taking a few shoot tips from each plant to encourage bushy growth, or harvest entire plants if lots of leaves are needed to make pesto or sauce.

How to Harvest

  • Regular picking: Pick the tips of the shoots regularly to keep basil plants bushy and productive
  • Microgreens: Harvest basil seedlings after only a couple of weeks, when packed with flavour and nutrients
  • Flowers: Basil flowers are also edible, with a milder flavour than the leaves

Using Your Basil

You can use your home-grown basil in many ways ‚Äì add the leaves to salads and use sweet basil to make pesto, tomato-based dishes or even to flavour ice cream. Thai basil brings rich flavours to Asian cuisine. Basil tends to lose its flavour if cooked, so just scatter fresh leaves over hot dishes right before serving.

Common Problems

Basil is generally easy to grow, but there are a few issues to watch out for:

Pests

  • Slugs and snails: These can damage young plants. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls
  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth. Remove by hand or use organic sprays

Diseases

  • Fungal diseases: When growing basil in a greenhouse, ventilate well to help reduce problems with fungal diseases
  • Damping off: Avoid overwatering seedlings to prevent this fungal disease

Environmental Issues

  • Cold damage: Basil is tender and needs plenty of warmth and sun. Keep young plants indoors until after the last frost
  • Early flowering: To prolong the leafy harvests, remove any flowers that start to form
  • End of season: At the end of the season, bring plants indoors or cover with cloches if you want to keep them going into autumn
