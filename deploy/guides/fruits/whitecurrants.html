Grow Your Own Whitecurrants

Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Whitecurrants in the UK

Getting Started

Whitecurrants are attractive, easy-to-grow soft fruit bushes that produce clusters of translucent white berries. They're essentially a colour variant of redcurrants but are sweeter and can be eaten fresh, making them more versatile than their red counterparts. They're hardy, self-fertile, and relatively trouble-free, making them excellent choices for UK gardens.

Key Characteristics

  • Pollination: Self-fertile, so a single bush will produce fruits
  • Growth Habit: Compact bushes that can be grown as bushes, standards, or cordons
  • Harvesting: Fruits ripen in mid to late summer (July-August) in long clusters (strigs)
  • Pruning: Prune in winter to maintain an open, goblet-shaped bush
  • Use: Can be eaten fresh (sweeter than redcurrants), cooked, or used for making jellies and preserves
  • Hardiness: Fully hardy in UK conditions

They're excellent for small gardens as they're compact and can be trained as cordons against walls or fences. They're also very hardy and reliable, producing good crops even in less-than-ideal conditions.

Choosing Varieties

While there are fewer whitecurrant varieties available compared to redcurrants, the main types offer different characteristics. All are self-fertile, so you only need one bush. Whitecurrants are essentially colour variants of redcurrants, so many varieties are similar to their red counterparts.

Factors to Consider

  • Fruit Size: Some varieties produce larger berries than others
  • Flavour: All are sweeter than redcurrants, but some are considered better for eating fresh
  • Disease Resistance: Some varieties show better resistance to common problems
  • Growth Habit: Most are similar, but consider if you want a bush or standard form
  • Harvest Time: Most ripen in July-August, but timing can vary slightly

Planting

Proper planting sets the foundation for healthy, productive whitecurrant bushes. Choose the right site, prepare the soil well, and plant at the correct time and depth.

Site Selection

  • Sunlight: Full sun is ideal, though whitecurrants tolerate light shade
  • Soil: Well-drained, fertile soil with pH 6.0-7.0. Whitecurrants tolerate a range of soil types
  • Shelter: Protect from strong winds, especially important for standard forms
  • Space: Allow adequate space for the mature bush size (1-1.5m height and spread)
  • Position: Can be grown as free-standing bushes, standards, or trained as cordons against walls/fences

Planting Bare-Root Bushes

  1. Timing: Plant during dormancy (November-March), avoiding frozen or waterlogged soil
  2. Preparation: Soak roots in water for 1-2 hours before planting
  3. Hole: Dig a hole wide enough to spread roots comfortably (at least 40cm wide), depth to match soil mark on stem
  4. Soil Improvement: Mix well-rotted compost or manure with excavated soil
  5. Planting: Position bush with roots spread out, ensuring the soil mark on stem is 5cm deeper than it was in the nursery
  6. Backfill: Fill hole, firming soil gently around roots to eliminate air pockets
  7. Staking: For standard forms, insert stake and tie securely
  8. Pruning: Cut back all shoots to 2-3 buds above ground after planting
  9. Watering: Water thoroughly after planting

Planting Container-Grown Bushes

  • Can be planted year-round, but autumn-winter is still best
  • Tease out any circling roots before planting
  • Plant 5cm deeper than in the container to encourage new shoots from the base
  • Water regularly, especially if planting in spring or summer
  • Prune back shoots to 2-3 buds after planting

Spacing Guidelines

  • Bush forms: 1.5m apart
  • Standard forms: 1.5m apart
  • Cordons: 40-50cm apart
üí° Pro Tip: Plant whitecurrants 5cm deeper than they were in the nursery or container. This encourages new shoots to grow from below ground, creating a bushier plant with more fruiting wood.

Plant Care

Consistent care throughout the growing season ensures healthy growth, good yields, and high-quality fruits. Whitecurrants require attention to watering, feeding, and winter pruning to maintain productive bushes.

Watering

  • Establishment: Water regularly during the first year, especially in dry periods
  • Growing Season: Water during dry spells, particularly when fruits are developing (June-August)
  • Method: Water at the base of plants, avoiding wetting the foliage
  • Mulching: Apply organic mulch around plants to conserve moisture and suppress weeds

Feeding

  • Spring: Apply balanced fertiliser (e.g., Growmore) or well-rotted manure in early spring
  • Potassium: Apply sulphate of potash in late winter to improve fruit quality
  • Frequency: Feed annually for best growth and fruiting
  • Container Plants: Feed regularly with liquid fertiliser during the growing season

Pruning

Whitecurrants produce fruit on old wood (2-3 year old branches) and at the base of new growth. Pruning maintains an open, goblet-shaped bush and encourages new fruiting wood.

Formative Pruning (First 3 Years)

  • Year 1: After planting, cut all shoots back to 2-3 buds above ground
  • Year 2: In winter, select 6-8 strong shoots to form the framework, cut back by half
  • Year 3: Continue building framework, remove weak or crossing branches
  • Goal: Create an open, goblet-shaped bush with 8-10 main branches

Maintenance Pruning (Established Bushes)

  • Timing: Prune in winter (November-March) when dormant
  • Remove: Dead, diseased, or damaged wood
  • Thin: Remove old, unproductive branches (4+ years old) to encourage new growth
  • Shape: Maintain open, goblet shape by removing crossing or overcrowded branches
  • New Growth: Shorten new shoots by about a third to encourage branching
  • Keep: Maintain 8-10 main branches, replacing old ones with new shoots from the base

Pruning Cordons

  • Summer: Prune side shoots back to 5 leaves in summer (July-August)
  • Winter: Further shorten side shoots to 2-3 buds in winter
  • Leader: Allow main stem to grow to desired height, then prune to maintain

Mulching

  • Apply a 5-8cm layer of organic mulch (compost, well-rotted manure, or bark chips) around plants
  • Keep mulch away from the base of stems to prevent rot
  • Replenish annually in spring
  • Helps suppress weeds and conserve moisture

Netting

  • Birds: Net plants when fruits start to ripen to protect from birds
  • Timing: Apply netting in late June to early July
  • Method: Use fine mesh netting, ensure it's secure and doesn't trap birds

Harvesting

Whitecurrants are ready to harvest when fully coloured (translucent white or pale yellow) and easily come away from the plant. They're typically harvested as whole clusters (strigs) rather than individual berries.

When to Harvest

  • Colour: Fruits should be fully white or pale yellow when ripe (usually mid to late July)
  • Feel: Berries should be firm and come away easily from the strig
  • Taste: Taste one to check - they should be sweet and fully flavoured
  • Timing: Harvest when all berries in a strig are ripe - they ripen together
  • Season: Typically July to early August, depending on variety
  • Weather: Harvest in dry weather, preferably in the morning when cool

Harvesting Method

  • Whole Strigs: Harvest entire clusters (strigs) rather than individual berries
  • Method: Gently pull or cut the strig from the plant, leaving the stalk attached
  • Container: Use shallow containers to avoid crushing fruits
  • Staged Harvesting: Strigs ripen at different times - harvest over several weeks
  • Gentle Handling: Handle strigs carefully to avoid berries falling off

Storage

  • Short-term: Store in the refrigerator for up to a week, keeping strigs intact
  • Freezing: Whitecurrants freeze well - freeze whole strigs or strip berries and freeze
  • Preserving: Excellent for making jellies, jams, and preserves
  • Fresh Eating: Can be eaten fresh - they're sweeter than redcurrants

Using Whitecurrants

  • Fresh Eating: Can be eaten fresh - sweeter and less tart than redcurrants
  • Cooking: Excellent for cooking and preserving
  • Jellies: High in pectin, making them perfect for clear whitecurrant jelly
  • Desserts: Can be used in tarts, pies, and other desserts
  • Stripping: To remove berries from strigs, run a fork down the strig
üí° Pro Tip: Harvest whitecurrants as whole strigs - it's much easier than picking individual berries. The berries can be stripped from the strigs later using a fork if needed. Whitecurrants are sweeter than redcurrants and can be eaten fresh, making them more versatile.

Common Problems

Whitecurrants are generally trouble-free, but they can occasionally be affected by some pests and diseases. Early identification and appropriate action help maintain healthy, productive bushes.

Pests

  • Birds: Can strip entire crops. Use netting to protect fruits, especially during ripening season.
  • Aphids: Can cause leaf curl and sooty mould. Usually not serious - encourage natural predators, use insecticidal soap if severe.
  • Currant Clearwing Moth: Larvae tunnel into stems, causing dieback. Remove and destroy affected stems, improve plant health.
  • Gooseberry Sawfly: Larvae can defoliate plants. Pick off by hand or use biological controls.

Diseases

  • Powdery Mildew: White, powdery coating on leaves and fruits. Improve air circulation, remove affected material, consider resistant varieties.
  • Leaf Spot: Brown spots on leaves. Usually not serious - remove affected leaves, improve air circulation.
  • Coral Spot: Red spots on dead wood. Prune out affected branches, maintain good plant health.
  • Rust: Orange spots on leaves. Remove affected leaves, improve air circulation.

Environmental Issues

  • Poor Fruit Set: Usually due to frost damage to flowers. Whitecurrants flower early, so protect from late frosts.
  • Drought Stress: Can cause poor fruit development. Water regularly during dry periods, especially when fruits are developing.
  • Overcrowding: Too many branches reduce air circulation and increase disease risk. Prune to maintain open shape.
  • Frost Damage: Early flowers can be damaged by late frosts. Protect with fleece if frost is forecast during flowering.

Prevention Strategies

  • Good Hygiene: Remove and destroy all pruned material and fallen fruits
  • Air Circulation: Maintain proper spacing and pruning to ensure good air flow
  • Healthy Stock: Buy healthy plants from reputable suppliers
  • Proper Pruning: Correct pruning reduces disease risk and improves plant health
  • Netting: Protect fruits from birds with netting during ripening
üí° Prevention Tips: Maintain good plant health through proper feeding and watering, ensure good air circulation through correct pruning, practice good garden hygiene by removing all pruned material and fallen fruits, protect flowers from late frosts, and net plants to protect fruits from birds.
