Getting Started

Gages (also called greengages) are a type of plum (Prunus domestica) that produce small to medium-sized fruits with distinctive green or yellow skin and an exceptionally sweet, honey-like flavour. These hardy, productive trees are well-suited to UK gardens and are particularly valued for their delicious flavour when eaten fresh. Gages are similar to plums in their growing requirements but are generally considered to have superior flavour.

Choosing Varieties

When choosing gage varieties, consider pollination requirements, harvest time, and flavour. Most gage varieties need a pollination partner (usually another gage or compatible plum variety), though some self-fertile varieties are available. Gages are particularly valued for their exceptional flavour, which is often described as the finest of all plums.

Factors to Consider

  • Pollination: Most gage varieties need a pollination partner (usually another gage or compatible plum variety). Plant compatible varieties within 50m (160ft) of each other, or choose self-fertile varieties.
  • Flavour: Gages are renowned for their exceptional, honey-like sweetness. All varieties have excellent flavour, but some are considered superior.
  • Harvest Time: Most gages ripen from late July to September. Choose varieties that suit your needs.
  • Fruit Size: Gages are generally smaller than dessert plums, but some varieties produce larger fruits than others.
  • Reliability: Some varieties are more reliable croppers than others in UK conditions.
  • Disease Resistance: Look for varieties with good resistance to common problems like bacterial canker and silver leaf.

Planting

Gages are best planted during the dormant season, from late autumn to early spring. Bare-root trees are available from November to March, while container-grown trees can be planted year-round, though autumn and spring are still best for establishment.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny position for best fruit production, though gages will tolerate partial shade. Avoid waterlogged sites and frost pockets. Most soil types are suitable, but well-drained, moisture-retentive, fertile soil is ideal. Gages prefer a pH of 6.0‚Äì7.0, though they're reasonably tolerant of different soil types. Good air circulation helps reduce disease problems. South- or west-facing walls are ideal for fan-trained trees.

Planting Bare-Root Trees

  1. Prepare the site: Dig a hole at least 60cm (2ft) wide and deep, breaking up the soil at the bottom. Mix in well-rotted organic matter or compost. Ensure the site is free from perennial weeds.
  2. Check the roots: Soak bare roots in water for 30 minutes before planting. Trim any damaged or broken roots back to healthy tissue.
  3. Plant at correct depth: Position the tree so the soil mark on the stem (where it was previously planted) is level with the soil surface. Spread roots out evenly in the hole. The graft union (the bulge where the variety joins the rootstock) should be well above soil level.
  4. Backfill and firm: Fill the hole with soil, firming gently as you go to eliminate air pockets. Create a slight depression around the tree to help water reach the roots.
  5. Stake and water: Insert a stake on the windward side and tie the tree loosely with a tree tie. Water thoroughly and mulch with organic matter, keeping it away from the trunk.

Planting Container-Grown Trees

Plant container-grown trees at the same depth they were in the pot. Gently tease out any roots that are circling the root ball. Water well after planting and keep well-watered during the first growing season, especially during dry spells.

Spacing

Space trees 4‚Äì5m (13‚Äì16ft) apart to allow for mature growth and good air circulation. This spacing helps reduce disease problems and makes maintenance easier. For fan-trained trees, space 3‚Äì4m (10‚Äì13ft) apart.

Training as Fans

Fan-trained gages against walls or fences are ideal for smaller gardens and provide protection from frost. Fans have a short trunk topped with a flat fan of radiating branches. They can be kept to 2‚Äì3m (6‚Äì10ft) tall and 3‚Äì4m (10‚Äì13ft) wide. Start training in the first year after planting.

üí° Planting Tip: For best results, plant gages in autumn so roots can establish before spring growth. If planting in spring, ensure trees are well-watered throughout the first summer. Always plant with the graft union well above soil level. Most gages need a pollination partner, so check requirements when choosing varieties. Fan-trained trees against walls benefit from extra heat and protection.

Plant Care

Gages need regular care to produce healthy crops. Key tasks include watering, feeding, mulching, and pruning. With proper care, trees will reward you with abundant harvests of delicious fruits for many years.

Watering

Water newly planted trees regularly during the first two years, especially during dry spells. Established trees are more drought-tolerant but will benefit from watering during prolonged dry periods, particularly when fruits are swelling (usually July to August). Water at the base of the tree, avoiding wetting the foliage to reduce disease risk.

Feeding

Feed gage trees in late winter with a balanced fertiliser or well-rotted organic matter. Apply a high-potassium fertiliser in spring to encourage fruiting. Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes leafy growth at the expense of fruit. Mulch annually in spring with well-rotted organic matter, keeping it away from the trunk. This helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and gradually improves soil structure.

Pruning

Prune gages in summer (after fruiting) to minimize the risk of silver leaf disease and bacterial canker. Avoid pruning in autumn and winter when these diseases are more active.

  • Formative pruning: In the first few years, establish a balanced framework of branches. For bush trees, select a central leader and 4‚Äì5 evenly spaced lateral branches.
  • Established trees: Prune after fruiting (late summer) to maintain shape and encourage new growth. Remove dead, diseased, or crossing branches. Thin out overcrowded growth to allow light and air to reach all parts of the tree.
  • Summer pruning: For fan-trained trees, prune in summer to maintain the fan shape and encourage fruiting spurs. This also helps control vigour.
  • Renovation: Neglected trees can be renovated over several years by removing dead wood and gradually reshaping the canopy. Avoid removing more than one-third of the tree in any one year.

Thinning

In early summer (June), thin out excess fruitlets to improve fruit size and quality. Leave one fruit per cluster, spacing them 5‚Äì8cm (2‚Äì3in) apart. This also helps prevent branches from breaking under heavy fruit loads and reduces the risk of biennial bearing.

Pollination

Most gage varieties need cross-pollination with another compatible variety (usually another gage or compatible plum) flowering at the same time. Plant compatible varieties within 50m (160ft) of each other, or choose self-fertile varieties. Bees and other pollinators are essential for good fruit set, so avoid using pesticides during flowering.

Pruning Timing: Gages should be pruned in summer (after fruiting) to minimize the risk of silver leaf disease and bacterial canker, which are more active in autumn and winter. Avoid pruning in autumn and winter. If you must prune in winter, seal all cuts with wound paint to prevent disease entry.

Harvesting

Gages are ready to harvest from late July to September, depending on variety. Ripe fruits are fully coloured (green or yellow depending on variety), slightly soft to the touch, and come away easily from the tree with a gentle twist. They should have a sweet, honey-like flavour. Fruits don't all ripen at once, so harvest over several weeks.

How to Harvest

  • Check ripeness: Ripe gages are fully coloured (green or yellow), slightly soft to the touch, and detach easily from the tree with a gentle twist. They should have a sweet, honey-like flavour and be juicy. Test a few fruits before harvesting the whole crop.
  • Harvest carefully: Handle gages gently to avoid bruising. Pick with the stalk attached for better storage. Place in shallow containers to prevent them from being crushed. Don't stack fruits too deeply.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick fruits every few days during the harvest season, as they ripen over several weeks. Check trees regularly when fruits are ripening. Harvest in the morning when fruits are cool and firm.
  • Use promptly: Fresh gages don't store well and are best eaten within a few days of harvest. Use or process promptly for best quality.

Storage

Fresh gages don't store well and are best eaten within 2‚Äì3 days of harvest. Store in the refrigerator in a shallow container, but don't wash until just before eating. For longer-term storage, gages can be frozen (freeze on trays first, then transfer to bags), made into jams, jellies, or preserves, or used in cooking and baking. Gages are particularly good for preserving due to their excellent flavour.

Using Your Gages

Fresh gages are delicious eaten raw, with their exceptional, honey-like sweetness making them one of the finest fruits for fresh eating. They're also excellent for cooking, making wonderful pies, crumbles, tarts, and sauces. Gages can be used in jams, jellies, and preserves, where their excellent flavour really shines. They can also be used in desserts and baking. The fruits are rich in vitamins A and C, and are a good source of dietary fibre and antioxidants.

üí° Harvesting Tip: Gages are at their best when fully ripe - they should be slightly soft and come away easily from the tree. Their exceptional flavour makes them perfect for eating fresh, though they can also be used for cooking and preserving. If you have a large crop, freeze excess fruits on trays first (so they don't stick together), then transfer to freezer bags. This allows you to enjoy their delicious flavour throughout the year.

Common Problems

Gages can be affected by various pests and diseases, similar to plums. With good cultural practices and appropriate site selection, most problems can be managed effectively. The main challenges are ensuring adequate pollination and protecting early flowers from frost.

Pests

  • Birds: Birds are attracted to ripening fruits. Protect trees with netting before fruits start to ripen. Ensure netting is well-secured and doesn't trap birds. Alternatively, grow trees in a fruit cage.
  • Plum moth: Larvae feed inside developing fruits, causing them to be maggoty. Remove and destroy affected fruits. Good hygiene and regular harvesting help reduce problems.
  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Usually not serious, but can distort leaves and spread viruses. Encourage natural predators or use organic sprays if necessary.
  • Winter moth: Caterpillars can defoliate trees. Hand-pick when possible, or use biological controls. Encourage birds which will help control caterpillar populations.

Diseases

  • Bacterial canker: Causes sunken, dead areas on branches and trunk, often with gum oozing from the wounds. Prune out affected areas, cutting back to healthy wood. Avoid pruning in autumn and winter when the disease is more active. Seal all cuts with wound paint.
  • Silver leaf: A serious fungal disease causing silvery leaves and branch dieback. Prune in summer to minimize risk (avoid autumn/winter pruning). Remove and destroy affected branches promptly. Seal all cuts with wound paint.
  • Plum leaf spot: Causes brown spots on leaves, which can lead to defoliation. Remove and destroy affected leaves. Ensure good air circulation through proper pruning. More common in wet conditions.
  • Brown rot: Causes fruits to rot and develop brown patches. Remove and destroy affected fruits promptly. Ensure good air circulation and avoid overcrowding. Harvest regularly to prevent overripe fruits from rotting.
  • Blossom wilt: Causes flowers and young fruits to wither and die. Remove affected parts promptly. Ensure good air circulation and avoid overhead watering.

Environmental Issues

  • Poor fruiting: Can be caused by lack of pollination, frost damage to flowers, or nutrient imbalances. Most gages need a pollination partner, so ensure compatible varieties are nearby. Young trees may take a few years to start fruiting well.
  • Frost damage: Early-flowering gages can be vulnerable to spring frosts. Choose warm, sheltered sites. Cover trees with fleece when frost is forecast during flowering.
  • Fruits splitting: Can be caused by irregular watering or heavy rain after dry periods. Ensure consistent watering, especially when fruits are swelling. Some varieties are more prone to splitting than others.
  • Biennial bearing: Trees produce heavy crops one year and light crops the next. Prevent by thinning fruits in heavy-crop years. Some varieties are more prone to this than others.
