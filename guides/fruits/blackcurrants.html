Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Blackcurrants in the UK

Getting Started

Blackcurrants (Ribes nigrum) are hardy, productive shrubs that produce delicious, vitamin C-rich berries perfect for eating fresh, cooking, or preserving. These easy-to-grow plants are well-suited to UK gardens and produce abundant harvests from early to mid-summer. Blackcurrants are particularly valued for their high vitamin C content and distinctive tart flavour, making them excellent for jams, jellies, cordials, and desserts.

Key Characteristics:

  • Hardy, deciduous shrubs reaching about 1.5m (5ft) in height and width
  • Multiple stems arising from the base, producing fruit on one- and two-year-old wood
  • Fruits ripen from early to mid-July, depending on variety
  • Self-fertile, so only one plant is needed (though more plants increase yields)
  • Very high in vitamin C and antioxidants
  • Excellent for jams, jellies, cordials, and cooking

Blackcurrants are native to northern Europe and are perfectly adapted to UK growing conditions. They're one of the easiest soft fruits to grow, requiring minimal maintenance once established. Modern varieties like 'Ben Connan' and 'Big Ben' offer larger fruits, better disease resistance, and improved flavour compared to older cultivars.

These plants are very productive, with a single bush capable of producing 3‚Äì5kg (6‚Äì11lb) of fruit each year. They're tolerant of most soil types and positions, though they produce best in full sun. Blackcurrants are particularly useful for smaller gardens, as they can be grown as bushes or trained as cordons against walls or fences.

üí° Quick Tip: Plant blackcurrants 5cm (2in) deeper than they were in the nursery to encourage new shoots from the base. This helps maintain a productive, multi-stemmed bush. Most varieties are self-fertile, so you only need one plant, though having more can increase yields through better pollination and provide a longer harvest period.

Choosing Varieties

When choosing blackcurrant varieties, consider fruit size, flavour, disease resistance, and harvest time. Modern 'Ben' series varieties offer improved characteristics including larger fruits, better disease resistance, and more reliable cropping. All varieties are self-fertile, so you only need one plant, though having more can increase yields.

Factors to Consider

  • Fruit Size: Modern varieties like 'Big Ben' produce much larger fruits than older cultivars, making them easier to harvest and more appealing.
  • Disease Resistance: Look for varieties with good resistance to common problems like gall midge, mildew, and leaf spot. 'Ben Hope' and 'Ben Connan' have excellent disease resistance.
  • Harvest Time: Early varieties like 'Ben Connan' ripen in early July, while later varieties extend the harvest into August. Choose varieties that suit your needs.
  • Flavour: Some varieties are sweeter and more suitable for eating fresh, while others are better for cooking and preserving. Most have the characteristic tart blackcurrant flavour.
  • Vigour: Some varieties are more compact and suitable for smaller gardens or containers, while others are more vigorous.
  • Yield: Modern varieties typically produce heavier crops than older cultivars. 'Big Ben' is particularly productive.

Planting

Blackcurrants are best planted during the dormant season, from late autumn to early spring. Bare-root plants are available from October to March, while container-grown plants can be planted year-round, though autumn and spring are still best for establishment.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny position for best fruit production, though blackcurrants will tolerate partial shade. Avoid waterlogged sites and frost pockets. Most soil types are suitable, but well-drained, moisture-retentive, fertile soil is ideal. Blackcurrants prefer a pH of 6.0‚Äì6.5, though they're reasonably tolerant of different soil types. Good air circulation helps reduce disease problems.

Planting Bare-Root Plants

  1. Prepare the site: Dig a hole at least 45cm (18in) wide and deep. Break up the soil at the bottom and mix in well-rotted organic matter or compost. Ensure the site is free from perennial weeds.
  2. Check the roots: Soak bare roots in water for 30 minutes before planting. Trim any damaged or broken roots back to healthy tissue.
  3. Plant at correct depth: Position the plant so it sits 5cm (2in) deeper than it was in the nursery (look for the soil mark on the stem). This encourages new shoots from the base. Spread roots out evenly in the hole.
  4. Backfill and firm: Fill the hole with soil, firming gently as you go to eliminate air pockets. Create a slight depression around the plant to help water reach the roots.
  5. Water and prune: Water thoroughly after planting. Prune all stems back to 5‚Äì10cm (2‚Äì4in) above ground level to encourage strong new growth from the base.

Planting Container-Grown Plants

Plant container-grown plants 5cm (2in) deeper than they were in the pot. Gently tease out any roots that are circling the root ball. Water well after planting and keep well-watered during the first growing season, especially during dry spells. Prune stems back as for bare-root plants.

Spacing

Space plants 1.5m (5ft) apart to allow for growth and good air circulation. This spacing helps reduce disease problems and makes maintenance easier. For cordon-trained plants, space 60cm (2ft) apart in rows 1.5m (5ft) apart.

Container Growing

Compact varieties like 'Ben Sarek' can be grown in large containers (at least 45cm / 18in diameter). Use a good-quality, moisture-retentive compost. Ensure containers have good drainage. Position in full sun and water regularly, as containers dry out faster than ground-planted specimens. Feed regularly during the growing season.

üí° Planting Tip: Always plant blackcurrants 5cm (2in) deeper than they were previously planted. This encourages new shoots from below ground level, which helps maintain a productive, multi-stemmed bush. Prune all stems back hard after planting to encourage strong new growth. For best results, plant in autumn so roots can establish before spring growth.

Plant Care

Blackcurrants need regular care to produce healthy crops. Key tasks include watering, feeding, mulching, and pruning. With proper care, plants will reward you with abundant harvests for many years.

Watering

Water newly planted bushes regularly during the first year, especially during dry spells. Established plants are more drought-tolerant but will benefit from watering during prolonged dry periods, particularly when fruits are swelling (usually June to July). Water at the base of plants, avoiding wetting the foliage to reduce disease risk. Container-grown plants need more frequent watering than those in the ground.

Feeding

Feed blackcurrants in early spring with a balanced fertiliser or well-rotted organic matter. Apply a high-potassium fertiliser in spring to encourage fruiting. Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes leafy growth at the expense of fruit. Mulch annually in spring with well-rotted organic matter, keeping it away from the base of stems. This helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and gradually improves soil structure.

Pruning

Pruning is essential for maintaining healthy, productive bushes. Blackcurrants fruit best on one- and two-year-old wood, so regular pruning encourages new growth and maintains productivity.

  • After planting: Prune all stems back to 5‚Äì10cm (2‚Äì4in) above ground level to encourage strong new growth from the base.
  • Established bushes: In winter, remove about one-third of the oldest stems at ground level. These are the thickest, darkest stems. This encourages new shoots from the base.
  • Maintenance pruning: Remove any dead, diseased, or damaged stems. Thin out overcrowded growth to allow light and air to reach all parts of the bush.
  • Renovation: Neglected bushes can be renovated by cutting all stems back to ground level in winter. This will result in no crop the following year, but the bush will regenerate with strong new growth.

Mulching

Mulch annually in spring with well-rotted organic matter, keeping it away from the base of stems to prevent rot. Mulching helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and gradually improves soil structure. Apply a layer 5‚Äì7cm (2‚Äì3in) deep around the base of plants.

Weed Control

Keep the area around plants free from weeds, especially during the first year. Mulching helps suppress weeds. Avoid deep cultivation near plants, as blackcurrants have shallow roots that can be damaged. Hand-weed or use a hoe carefully around plants.

Pruning System: Blackcurrants fruit best on one- and two-year-old wood. By removing about one-third of the oldest stems each winter, you encourage new growth from the base, which will fruit in the following years. This maintains a productive bush with a good mix of young and older stems. Always remove the thickest, darkest stems, as these are the oldest.

Harvesting

Blackcurrants are ready to harvest from early to mid-July, depending on variety. Ripe fruits are fully black, plump, and come away easily from the plant. They should have a sweet-tart flavour and be juicy. Fruits don't all ripen at once, so harvest over several weeks.

How to Harvest

  • Check ripeness: Ripe blackcurrants are fully black, plump, and come away easily from the plant. They should be juicy and have a sweet-tart flavour. Test a few fruits before harvesting the whole crop.
  • Harvest methods: You can pick individual fruits or cut entire clusters (strigs) with scissors or secateurs. Cutting strigs is faster and less damaging to the plant. Pick into shallow containers to prevent fruits from being crushed.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick fruits every few days during the harvest season, as they ripen quickly. Check plants regularly when fruits are ripening. Harvest in the morning when fruits are cool and firm.
  • Use promptly: Fresh blackcurrants don't store well and are best used within a few days of harvest. Use or process promptly for best quality.

Storage

Fresh blackcurrants don't store well and are best used within 2‚Äì3 days of harvest. Store in the refrigerator in a shallow container, but don't wash until just before using. For longer-term storage, blackcurrants can be frozen (freeze on trays first, then transfer to bags), made into jams, jellies, cordials, or preserves, or used in cooking and baking.

Using Your Blackcurrants

Fresh blackcurrants are delicious eaten raw, though their tart flavour means they're often better when sweetened or cooked. They're excellent for cooking, making wonderful pies, crumbles, tarts, and sauces. Blackcurrants are particularly valued for making jams, jellies, and cordials, as they have a high pectin content and distinctive flavour. They can be used in smoothies, yoghurts, and desserts. The fruits are very rich in vitamin C and antioxidants, making them particularly nutritious. Blackcurrant cordial is a traditional and delicious way to preserve the harvest.

üí° Harvesting Tip: Harvest blackcurrants by cutting entire clusters (strigs) with scissors or secateurs. This is faster and less damaging than picking individual fruits. Strip the fruits from the strigs after harvesting. If you have a large crop, freeze excess fruits on trays first (so they don't stick together), then transfer to freezer bags. This allows you to use them in smaller portions throughout the year.

Common Problems

Blackcurrants are generally trouble-free, but they can be affected by various pests and diseases. With good cultural practices and appropriate variety selection, most problems can be managed effectively.

Pests

  • Birds: Birds are attracted to ripening fruits. Protect plants with netting before fruits start to ripen. Ensure netting is well-secured and doesn't trap birds. Alternatively, grow plants in a fruit cage.
  • Blackcurrant gall midge: Larvae feed inside leaves, causing them to curl and distort. Remove and destroy affected leaves. Choose resistant varieties like 'Ben Hope' and 'Ben Connan'.
  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Usually not serious, but can distort leaves and spread viruses. Encourage natural predators or use organic sprays if necessary.
  • Big bud mite: Causes buds to swell and fail to open. Remove and destroy affected buds. Choose resistant varieties. This pest can also spread reversion virus.
  • Capsid bugs: Can cause distorted leaves and fruits. Usually not serious, but can affect fruit quality. Encourage natural predators.

Diseases

  • Powdery mildew: Causes white, powdery coating on leaves and stems. More common in dry conditions. Ensure adequate watering and choose resistant varieties like 'Ben Hope'. Remove and destroy affected parts.
  • Leaf spot: Causes brown spots on leaves, which can lead to defoliation. Remove and destroy affected leaves. Ensure good air circulation through proper pruning. More common in wet conditions.
  • Reversion virus: A serious disease spread by big bud mite. Causes leaves to become narrower and plants to become less productive. There's no cure, so remove and destroy affected plants. Buy certified virus-free plants from reputable suppliers.
  • Botrytis (grey mould): Causes fruits to rot, especially in wet weather. Remove affected fruits promptly. Ensure good air circulation and avoid overcrowding. Harvest regularly to prevent overripe fruits from rotting.
  • Coral spot: Causes dieback of stems, with coral-pink pustules on dead wood. Prune out affected stems, cutting back to healthy wood. More common on stressed plants.

Environmental Issues

  • Poor fruiting: Can be caused by lack of pruning, poor pollination, or nutrient imbalances. Ensure proper pruning to maintain productive stems, feed appropriately, and ensure good growing conditions. Young plants may take a year or two to start fruiting well.
  • Fruits not ripening: Can be caused by lack of sun, cool weather, or poor growing conditions. Ensure plants are in a sunny position and have adequate water and nutrients. Some varieties are more reliable than others.
  • Winter damage: Stems can be damaged by severe frosts, especially in exposed positions. Most varieties are hardy in the UK, but protect with horticultural fleece if severe frosts are forecast.
  • Drought stress: Can cause poor growth and reduced fruiting. Ensure adequate watering, especially during dry spells and when fruits are swelling. Mulching helps conserve moisture.

üí° Growing Tip: The key to success with blackcurrants is proper pruning and variety selection. Remove about one-third of the oldest stems each winter to encourage new growth. Choose modern varieties with good disease resistance, particularly to gall midge and mildew. Keep plants well-watered and fed, and ensure good air circulation through proper spacing and pruning. With good care, plants will produce abundant harvests for many years.
