Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Medlars in the UK

Getting Started

Medlars (Mespilus germanica) are attractive, ornamental trees that produce unique, edible fruits perfect for cooking, jams, and preserves. These hardy, deciduous trees are well-suited to UK gardens and are particularly valued for their ornamental beauty and unusual fruits. Medlars are closely related to apples and quinces and have been cultivated in the UK for centuries.

Key Characteristics:

  • Hardy, deciduous trees reaching 4‚Äì6m (13‚Äì20ft) in height
  • Self-fertile, so only one tree is needed
  • Fruits ripen in late autumn and need bletting (softening) before eating
  • Attractive white flowers in late spring and ornamental autumn foliage
  • Excellent for cooking, jams, and preserves; can be eaten fresh when bletted
  • Very hardy and trouble-free, making them ideal for UK gardens

Medlars are native to southwest Asia and southeastern Europe but have been grown in the UK for centuries. They're particularly valued for their ornamental beauty, with attractive white flowers in late spring and colourful autumn foliage. The fruits are unique - they're harvested in late autumn and need to be 'bletted' (softened) before eating, which makes them sweet and edible.

These trees are very hardy and relatively trouble-free, making them ideal for UK gardens. They're self-fertile, so you only need one tree, and they're tolerant of most soil types and positions. With proper care, a single tree can produce 10‚Äì20kg (22‚Äì44lb) of fruit each year. Medlars are particularly valued for their ornamental qualities and unusual fruits, which are excellent for cooking and preserving.

Choosing Varieties

When choosing medlar varieties, consider fruit size, flavour, and shape. All medlars are self-fertile, so you only need one tree. Look for varieties that are reliable croppers and have good flavour when bletted.

Factors to Consider

  • Fruit Size: Some varieties produce larger fruits than others. Larger fruits are often easier to handle and process.
  • Flavour: All medlars need bletting before eating, but some varieties have better flavour than others when bletted.
  • Fruit Shape: Some varieties have round fruits, while others have flatter fruits. Flatter fruits can be more prone to cracking.
  • Reliability: Some varieties are more reliable croppers than others in UK conditions.
  • Ornamental Value: All medlars are ornamental, but some have particularly attractive flowers or autumn foliage.
  • Hardiness: Most medlar varieties are very hardy, but some may be more tolerant of challenging conditions.

Planting

Medlars are best planted during the dormant season, from late autumn to early spring. Bare-root trees are available from November to March, while container-grown trees can be planted year-round, though autumn and spring are still best for establishment.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny or partially shaded position. Medlars are very tolerant and will grow in most positions, but they produce best in full sun. Avoid waterlogged sites and frost pockets. Most soil types are suitable, but well-drained, moisture-retentive, fertile soil is ideal. Medlars prefer slightly acidic to neutral soil (pH 6.0‚Äì7.0), though they're very tolerant of different soil types. Good air circulation helps reduce disease problems.

Planting Bare-Root Trees

  1. Prepare the site: Dig a hole at least 60cm (2ft) wide and deep, breaking up the soil at the bottom. Mix in well-rotted organic matter or compost. Ensure the site is free from perennial weeds.
  2. Check the roots: Soak bare roots in water for 30 minutes before planting. Trim any damaged or broken roots back to healthy tissue.
  3. Plant at correct depth: Position the tree so the soil mark on the stem (where it was previously planted) is level with the soil surface. Spread roots out evenly in the hole. The graft union (if present) should be well above soil level.
  4. Backfill and firm: Fill the hole with soil, firming gently as you go to eliminate air pockets. Create a slight depression around the tree to help water reach the roots.
  5. Stake and water: Insert a stake on the windward side and tie the tree loosely with a tree tie. Water thoroughly and mulch with organic matter, keeping it away from the trunk.

Planting Container-Grown Trees

Plant container-grown trees at the same depth they were in the pot. Gently tease out any roots that are circling the root ball. Water well after planting and keep well-watered during the first growing season, especially during dry spells.

Spacing

Space trees 4‚Äì5m (13‚Äì16ft) apart to allow for mature growth and good air circulation. This spacing helps reduce disease problems and makes maintenance easier. Medlars can be kept smaller with pruning if space is limited.

Plant Care

Medlars need regular care to produce healthy crops. Key tasks include watering, feeding, mulching, and pruning. With proper care, trees will reward you with abundant harvests and ornamental beauty for many years.

Watering

Water newly planted trees regularly during the first two years, especially during dry spells. Established trees are more drought-tolerant but will benefit from watering during prolonged dry periods, particularly when fruits are developing (usually August to October). Water at the base of the tree, avoiding wetting the foliage to reduce disease risk.

Feeding

Feed medlar trees in late winter with a balanced fertiliser or well-rotted organic matter. Apply a high-potassium fertiliser in spring to encourage fruiting. Avoid excessive nitrogen, which promotes leafy growth at the expense of fruit. Mulch annually in spring with well-rotted organic matter, keeping it away from the trunk. This helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and gradually improves soil structure.

Pruning

Prune medlars in winter (when dormant) to maintain shape and encourage productive growth. The aim is to create an open-centred tree with good air circulation.

  • Formative pruning: In the first few years, establish a balanced framework of branches. Select a central leader and 4‚Äì5 evenly spaced lateral branches. Remove any weak, crossing, or overcrowded branches.
  • Established trees: Prune in winter to remove dead, diseased, or damaged wood. Thin out overcrowded growth to allow light and air to reach all parts of the tree. Maintain an open-centred structure.
  • Maintenance: Keep the framework open and well-spaced. Remove any branches growing into the centre. Medlars respond well to pruning and can be kept to a manageable size.
  • Renovation: Neglected trees can be renovated over several years by removing dead wood and gradually reshaping the canopy. Avoid removing more than one-third of the tree in any one year.

Mulching

Mulch annually in spring with well-rotted organic matter, keeping it away from the trunk to prevent rot. Mulching helps conserve moisture, suppress weeds, and gradually improves soil structure. Apply a layer 5‚Äì7cm (2‚Äì3in) deep around the base of the tree.

Pruning Timing: Medlars should be pruned in winter when the tree is dormant. This is the best time to see the tree's structure and make pruning decisions. Avoid pruning in spring when the tree is actively growing, as this can reduce fruiting. Summer pruning can be done if necessary to control growth, but winter pruning is preferred.

Harvesting

Medlars are ready to harvest in late October to November, usually after the first frosts. The fruits should come away easily from the tree with a gentle twist. Unlike most fruits, medlars are not eaten immediately after harvesting - they need to be 'bletted' (softened) first, which makes them sweet and edible.

How to Harvest

  • Check ripeness: Medlars are ready to harvest when they come away easily from the tree with a gentle twist. They're typically harvested in late October to November, often after the first frosts. The fruits should be firm but fully developed.
  • Harvest carefully: Handle medlars gently to avoid bruising. Pick with the stalk attached for better storage. Place in shallow containers to prevent them from being crushed. Don't stack fruits too deeply.
  • Harvest timing: Medlars are usually harvested after the first frosts, which helps with the bletting process. However, they can be harvested before frosts if necessary and will still blet successfully.
  • Bletting required: Unlike most fruits, medlars are not eaten immediately after harvesting. They need to be stored for 2‚Äì3 weeks until they become soft and brown (bletted) before eating.

Bletting Process

Bletting is the process of softening medlars after harvest. Store harvested fruits in a cool, dark place (such as a shed or garage) for 2‚Äì3 weeks. During this time, the fruits will soften and turn brown. They're ready to eat when they're soft to the touch and the flesh is brown and sweet. The bletting process breaks down the tannins and makes the fruits sweet and edible.

Storage

After bletting, medlars can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days, but they're best used promptly. For longer-term storage, medlars can be made into jams, jellies, or preserves. They can also be frozen after bletting. Unbletted fruits can be stored in a cool place for several weeks until they blet.

Using Your Medlars

Once bletted, medlars are delicious eaten fresh, with their sweet, unique flavour making them perfect for desserts and snacking. They're often paired with wine, port, and cheese. Medlars are also excellent for cooking, making wonderful jams, jellies, and preserves. They can be used in tarts, pies, and other desserts. The fruits are rich in vitamins and minerals, and are a good source of dietary fibre.

Common Problems

Medlars are generally very trouble-free and are resistant to most pests and diseases. They're particularly hardy and well-suited to UK conditions. With good cultural practices and appropriate site selection, most problems can be avoided or managed effectively.

Pests

  • Birds: Birds are attracted to ripening fruits. Protect trees with netting before fruits start to ripen. Ensure netting is well-secured and doesn't trap birds. Alternatively, grow trees in a fruit cage.
  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Usually not serious, but can distort leaves and spread viruses. Encourage natural predators or use organic sprays if necessary.
  • Winter moth: Caterpillars can defoliate trees. Hand-pick when possible, or use biological controls. Encourage birds which will help control caterpillar populations.

Diseases

  • Fireblight: A serious bacterial disease that can affect medlars (though they're less susceptible than some other fruit trees). Causes branches to die back with blackened leaves. Prune out affected areas promptly, cutting back to healthy wood. Ensure good air circulation through proper pruning.
  • Leaf spot: Causes brown spots on leaves, which can lead to defoliation. Remove and destroy affected leaves. Ensure good air circulation through proper pruning. More common in wet conditions.
  • Brown rot: Causes fruits to rot and develop brown patches. Remove and destroy affected fruits promptly. Ensure good air circulation and avoid overcrowding. Harvest regularly to prevent overripe fruits from rotting.

Environmental Issues

  • Poor fruiting: Can be caused by lack of pruning, nutrient imbalances, or young age. Ensure trees are properly pruned and fed. Young trees may take a few years to start fruiting well. Medlars are self-fertile, so pollination isn't usually a problem.
  • Frost damage: Early-flowering medlars can be vulnerable to spring frosts, though they're generally hardy. Choose sheltered sites if possible. Cover trees with fleece when frost is forecast during flowering.
  • Fruits not bletting: If fruits don't blet properly, ensure they're stored in a cool, dark place. The bletting process can take 2‚Äì3 weeks. Some varieties may take longer than others.
