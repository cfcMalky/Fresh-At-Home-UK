Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Mint in the UK

Getting Started

Mint (Mentha) is one of the easiest and most popular herbs to grow, with a refreshing, aromatic flavour that's essential in many dishes and drinks. This hardy perennial herb spreads vigorously and will return year after year, making it a valuable addition to any UK garden. However, its spreading habit means it's best grown in containers to prevent it from taking over.

Key Characteristics:

  • Hardy perennial that returns year after year
  • Grows 30‚Äì90cm (1‚Äì3ft) tall with spreading habit
  • Produces spikes of small flowers in summer
  • Refreshing, aromatic flavour
  • Essential in cooking, drinks, and teas
  • Can spread vigorously if not contained

Mint is native to Europe, Asia, and Africa and has been cultivated for centuries for its culinary, medicinal, and aromatic properties. There are many varieties of mint, each with its own unique flavour‚Äîfrom the classic spearmint and peppermint to more exotic varieties like apple mint, chocolate mint, and pineapple mint. All varieties share the characteristic refreshing flavour and spreading growth habit.

One important thing to note about mint is that it can spread quite vigorously through its roots. For this reason, many gardeners prefer to grow it in containers or use root barriers to prevent it from taking over the garden. However, with proper management, mint is an excellent addition to any herb garden and provides a steady supply of fresh leaves throughout the growing season.

Choosing Varieties

There are many varieties of mint available, each with its own unique flavour and characteristics. The most common are spearmint and peppermint, but there are also many other interesting varieties to explore.

Mint is typically grown from seeds, cuttings, or division. When purchasing mint, you're usually buying seeds, small plants, or root divisions. The main consideration is which flavour you prefer, as different varieties have distinct tastes. You can also grow multiple varieties in separate containers to enjoy a range of flavours.

Recommended Varieties

Spearmint

The most common variety (Mentha spicata). Classic mint flavour, perfect for cooking, drinks, and teas. Most widely available and reliable performer. Best choice for general use.

Peppermint

Stronger, more menthol flavour (Mentha √ó piperita). Excellent for teas and medicinal uses. More intense than spearmint. Good for containers.

Apple Mint

Milder flavour with a hint of apple (Mentha suaveolens). Less aggressive spreading than other mints. Good for cooking and teas. Attractive fuzzy leaves.

Chocolate Mint

Unique flavour with hints of chocolate (Mentha √ó piperita 'Chocolate'). Decorative and aromatic. Excellent for desserts and drinks. Less vigorous than other mints.

Sowing Mint

Sowing Outdoors

Mint can be sown directly outdoors from mid-spring to early summer. It's very easy to grow from seed and will germinate reliably. However, mint is more commonly grown from cuttings or division, as seeds can produce variable results.

  1. Prepare the site: Choose a sunny or partially shaded location with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. Remove weeds and rake the soil to a fine tilth.
  2. Sow seeds: Sow seeds thinly on the soil surface or cover very lightly (seeds need light to germinate). Space rows 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart.
  3. Water gently: Water the soil gently after sowing, taking care not to wash away the seeds. Keep the soil moist until germination.
  4. Thin seedlings: When seedlings are large enough to handle, thin them to 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart. Mint spreads, so give it plenty of space.

Sowing Indoors

You can start mint indoors in late winter or early spring for an earlier start. Sow seeds in pots or seed trays filled with peat-free seed compost. Cover seeds very lightly or not at all, as they need light to germinate. Keep at 15‚Äì20¬∞C (59‚Äì68¬∞F) and maintain consistent moisture. Seeds should germinate within 10‚Äì14 days. Transplant seedlings outdoors after the last frost, hardening them off first.

Growing from Cuttings (Recommended)

Mint is most commonly grown from cuttings, as this ensures you get the exact variety you want. Take cuttings from healthy plants in spring or summer. Cut a 10‚Äì15cm (4‚Äì6in) stem, remove the lower leaves, and place in water or directly in compost. Roots should develop within 1‚Äì2 weeks. This is the most reliable way to propagate mint.

However, mint is more reliably grown from cuttings or division, as seeds can produce variable results. If you want a specific variety, use cuttings or purchase plants.

Planting

Mint is very adaptable and will grow in a wide range of conditions. It prefers a sunny or partially shaded position with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. The main consideration is controlling its spread, so many gardeners prefer to grow it in containers.

Container Planting (Strongly Recommended)

Growing mint in containers is strongly recommended to control its spread. Use a container at least 30cm (12in) wide and deep with good drainage. Fill with peat-free multi-purpose compost. Container-grown mint will need more frequent watering than ground-grown plants. You can also grow different varieties in separate containers to enjoy a range of flavours.

Site Selection (Ground Planting)

If planting in the ground, choose a sunny or partially shaded location with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. Mint will tolerate most soil types, including clay, but prefers soil that doesn't become waterlogged. It will also grow in light shade. However, ground planting is only recommended if you use root barriers (at least 30cm / 12in deep) to prevent spread.

Ground Planting with Barriers

If you want to plant mint in the ground, it's essential to use root barriers to prevent it from spreading. Install a barrier (at least 30cm / 12in deep) around the planting area, or plant it in a bottomless container sunk into the ground. Space plants 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart if planting multiple varieties.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Mint can spread very vigorously through its roots and will quickly take over a garden if not contained. Always grow mint in containers or use root barriers if planting in the ground. Even with barriers, mint can escape, so container growing is strongly recommended.

Plant Care

Mint is very low-maintenance once established. It's a hardy perennial that will return year after year with minimal care. However, a few simple practices will help ensure healthy growth and prevent it from becoming too invasive.

Watering

Water mint regularly during dry spells, especially when plants are young. Once established, mint is quite drought-tolerant, but regular watering will encourage more vigorous growth and better leaf quality. Container-grown plants will need more frequent watering than those in the ground. Mint prefers consistently moist soil, so don't let it dry out completely. Water at the base of plants to avoid wetting the leaves, which can lead to fungal issues.

Feeding

Mint doesn't usually need additional feeding if grown in reasonably fertile soil. However, if your soil is poor, you can apply a balanced, organic fertiliser in spring. Container-grown plants may benefit from a light liquid feed every 4‚Äì6 weeks during the growing season. Avoid over-fertilising, as this can encourage excessive growth and make the plant more invasive.

Pruning and Cutting Back

Regular harvesting of leaves helps keep mint bushy and encourages new growth. Cut back plants by about one-third after flowering to encourage fresh growth. You can also cut back hard in early spring to remove old growth and encourage new shoots. Regular harvesting also helps prevent plants from becoming leggy.

Controlling Spread

Mint can spread very vigorously through its roots. To control spread, always grow it in containers or use root barriers if planting in the ground. Regularly check for roots escaping from containers and trim them back. If you're growing mint in the ground, consider installing a root barrier (at least 30cm / 12in deep) around the planting area. Alternatively, grow it in containers to completely control its spread.

Dividing

Mint benefits from being divided every 2‚Äì3 years to maintain vigour. Divide plants in spring or autumn by digging up the clump (or removing from container) and separating it into smaller sections. Replant the divisions immediately in fresh compost. This is also a great way to propagate new plants and share with friends.

Winter Care

Mint is fully hardy and will die back in winter, then regrow in spring. In autumn, cut back any dead foliage. The roots will survive underground and send up new growth in spring. Container-grown plants may benefit from some protection in very cold areas‚Äîmove containers to a sheltered spot or wrap them in fleece. In mild winters, mint may retain some leaves.

Harvesting

Mint leaves can be harvested as soon as plants are large enough, usually 6‚Äì8 weeks after planting. The leaves are best used fresh, as they lose much of their flavour when dried. However, they can be dried or frozen for later use. Harvest regularly to encourage new growth and keep plants bushy.

How to Harvest

  • Pick young leaves: Harvest leaves when they're young and tender for the best flavour. Older leaves can become tough and less flavourful.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick leaves regularly throughout the growing season to encourage new growth and prevent plants from becoming leggy.
  • Cut whole stems: You can cut whole stems near the base, or pick individual leaves as needed. Cutting stems encourages bushier growth.
  • Harvest before flowering: For the best flavour, harvest leaves before the plant flowers. However, the flowers are attractive to bees, so you may want to allow some to flower.

Using Your Mint

Fresh mint leaves add a refreshing, aromatic flavour to many dishes and drinks. Use them in salads, desserts, drinks, teas, and savoury dishes. They're excellent in Middle Eastern and Mediterranean cuisine, particularly with lamb, vegetables, and yogurt. Mint also makes a wonderful herbal tea, either on its own or mixed with other herbs. The leaves can be used to flavour drinks, syrups, and vinegars.

Drying Mint

While fresh leaves have the best flavour, mint can be dried for later use. Harvest leaves in the morning after the dew has dried. Dry them in a warm, well-ventilated area out of direct sunlight, or use a dehydrator. Store dried leaves in an airtight container in a cool, dark place. Dried mint retains some flavour but is not as aromatic as fresh leaves.

Freezing Mint

Mint can be frozen for use in cooked dishes and drinks. Freeze whole leaves on a tray, then transfer to freezer bags, or chop leaves and freeze in ice cube trays with a little water. Frozen mint is best used in cooked dishes and drinks rather than raw applications, as freezing can affect the texture.

Storage

Fresh mint leaves can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days. Place stems in a glass of water like cut flowers, or wrap in damp kitchen paper and store in the fridge. For longer storage, dry or freeze the leaves as described above. Dried mint can be stored in an airtight container for up to a year, though the flavour will diminish over time.

Common Problems

Mint is generally very trouble-free and rarely suffers from serious pests or diseases. However, there are a few issues to watch out for, mostly related to its vigorous growth habit.

Pests

  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Usually not serious and can be controlled by encouraging natural predators or using organic methods.
  • Slugs and snails: Can damage young leaves, especially in spring. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls. Once established, mint is less attractive to slugs.
  • Spider mites: Can occur in hot, dry conditions. Increase humidity and improve air circulation. Usually not serious if caught early.

Diseases

  • Powdery mildew: Can occur in humid conditions or when plants are overcrowded. Improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering. Usually not serious if caught early.
  • Rust: Fungal disease that can affect leaves, causing orange or brown pustules. Remove affected leaves and improve air circulation. Usually not serious.

Environmental Issues

  • Invasive spread: The main issue with mint is its tendency to spread. Always grow it in containers or use root barriers if planting in the ground. Regularly check for roots escaping from containers.
  • Leggy growth: Can occur if plants aren't pruned regularly. Cut back plants by about one-third after flowering and harvest leaves regularly to encourage bushy growth.
  • Poor flavour: Can occur if plants are stressed, over-fertilised, or harvested at the wrong time. Ensure adequate water, avoid over-fertilising, and harvest leaves before flowering for the best flavour.

The main challenge is controlling its spread, so always grow it in containers or use root barriers. With proper management, you'll have a steady supply of fresh, aromatic mint leaves for years to come!
