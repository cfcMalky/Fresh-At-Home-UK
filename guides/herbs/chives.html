Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Chives in the UK

Getting Started

Chives (Allium schoenoprasum) are one of the easiest and most versatile herbs to grow, providing a continuous supply of mild onion-flavoured leaves throughout the growing season. These hardy perennials are perfect for UK gardens, thriving in containers, borders, or even as an attractive edging plant. Chives are a must-have herb for any kitchen garden, offering both culinary value and ornamental appeal with their pretty purple flowers.

Key Characteristics:

  • Hardy perennial herb, easy to grow
  • Provides continuous harvests from spring to autumn
  • Perfect for containers, borders, or edging
  • Attractive purple flowers attract pollinators
  • Mild onion flavour, perfect for salads and cooking
  • Low-maintenance and very reliable

Chives are members of the onion family and are one of the most reliable herbs for UK gardens. They form neat clumps of hollow, grass-like leaves that can be harvested repeatedly throughout the growing season. The leaves have a mild, delicate onion flavour that's perfect for adding to salads, soups, egg dishes, and many other recipes without being overpowering.

In addition to their culinary value, chives produce attractive spherical purple flowers in early summer that are not only beautiful but also edible and attractive to bees and other beneficial insects. Chives are extremely low-maintenance once established and will return year after year, making them an excellent investment for any garden.

Choosing Varieties

While common chives (Allium schoenoprasum) are the most widely grown, there are a few varieties worth considering. Garlic chives (Allium tuberosum) are also popular and have a slightly different flavour profile, with flat leaves and white flowers.

Common chives have round, hollow leaves and purple flowers, while garlic chives have flat, solid leaves and white flowers with a more garlicky flavour. Both are easy to grow and have similar cultivation requirements, though garlic chives tend to be slightly less hardy.

Recommended Varieties

Common Chives

The standard variety with round, hollow leaves and purple flowers. Most widely available and reliable for UK growing. Perfect for all culinary uses.

Garlic Chives

Features flat, solid leaves with a garlicky flavour. White flowers appear later in summer. Slightly less hardy but still suitable for UK gardens.

'Forescate'

A variety of common chives with particularly large, flavourful leaves. Excellent for culinary use and reliable in UK conditions.

Sowing Chives

Sowing Indoors

For an early start, sow chives seeds indoors from late winter to early spring. Chives germinate easily and grow quickly, making them straightforward to start from seed.

  1. Prepare containers: Fill seed trays or small pots with peat-free seed compost. Chives seeds are small, so use fine compost.
  2. Sow seeds: Sow seeds thinly on the surface of the compost, or cover very lightly with a fine layer of vermiculite. Chives seeds need light to germinate.
  3. Provide warmth: Keep in a warm location (15‚Äì20¬∞C / 59‚Äì68¬∞F) such as on a windowsill or in a propagator. Germination usually takes 10‚Äì14 days.
  4. Water carefully: Water from below or use a fine mist to avoid disturbing the small seeds. Keep compost moist but not waterlogged.
  5. Pot on: When seedlings are large enough to handle, pot them into individual containers or thin to allow space for growth. Chives can be grown in clumps.

Sowing Outdoors

Chives can be sown directly outdoors from early spring to early summer. They're easy to grow and will establish quickly. For best results, sow in a prepared seedbed and transplant to their final position when large enough.

Sow seeds thinly in drills about 0.5cm (¬ºin) deep, spacing rows 30cm (12in) apart. Thin seedlings to 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart when they're large enough to handle. Alternatively, sow in small clumps and thin to leave the strongest seedlings in each position.

üí° Quick Start: If you want chives quickly, consider buying young plants from a garden centre. They'll establish faster than seeds and can be harvested within a few weeks of planting.

Planting

Chives are extremely adaptable and can be grown in containers, borders, raised beds, or even as an attractive edging plant. They prefer a sunny position but will tolerate partial shade, and they're not fussy about soil type as long as it's reasonably well-drained.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny location with well-drained soil. Chives prefer full sun but will grow in partial shade, though growth may be slightly slower. They're not fussy about soil type and will grow in most conditions, though they prefer reasonably fertile, moisture-retentive soil.

Container Planting

Chives are perfect for container growing, making them ideal for patios, balconies, and windowsills. Use pots at least 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) in diameter with good drainage. Fill with peat-free multi-purpose compost and plant seedlings or divisions 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart. Container-grown chives will need regular watering, especially during dry spells.

Ground Planting

If planting in the ground, prepare the soil by removing weeds and incorporating well-rotted compost if the soil is poor. Plant seedlings or divisions at the same depth they were growing previously, spacing them 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart. Water well after planting and keep the soil moist until plants are established.

üí° Edging Plant: Chives make excellent edging plants for vegetable gardens or herb borders. Their neat, clumping habit and attractive flowers add both function and beauty to the garden.

Plant Care

Chives are extremely low-maintenance once established. Regular watering, occasional feeding, and dividing every few years will keep plants healthy and productive. They're very reliable and will return year after year with minimal care.

Watering

Water chives regularly during dry spells, especially when plants are young or growing in containers. Once established, chives are quite drought-tolerant, but regular watering will encourage more vigorous growth and better harvests. Container-grown chives will need more frequent watering than those in the ground.

Feeding

Chives don't usually need additional feeding if grown in reasonably fertile soil. However, container-grown plants may benefit from a light liquid feed every 4‚Äì6 weeks during the growing season. Use a balanced, organic fertiliser at half strength. A spring mulch of well-rotted compost around ground-grown plants will help maintain soil fertility.

Dividing

Chives benefit from being divided every 3‚Äì4 years to maintain vigour. Divide in spring or autumn by lifting the clump and separating it into smaller sections, each with a few bulbs. Replant the divisions immediately, spacing them 15‚Äì20cm (6‚Äì8in) apart. This not only rejuvenates the plants but also provides you with more plants to expand your chive collection or share with friends.

Removing Flowers

Chives produce attractive purple (or white for garlic chives) flowers in early summer. While these are edible and attractive to pollinators, removing the flower stems as they appear will encourage the plant to produce more leaves. If you want to enjoy the flowers, allow some to bloom but remove others to maintain leaf production.

Winter Care

Chives are fully hardy in the UK and will survive winter outdoors without protection. The leaves die back in autumn and new growth appears in spring. In mild winters, you may get some growth throughout the year. Container-grown chives can be moved to a sheltered position if desired, but this isn't necessary.

Harvesting

Chives can be harvested as soon as the leaves are large enough, usually 6‚Äì8 weeks after planting. Harvest regularly throughout the growing season by cutting leaves from the outside of the clump, leaving the central growing point intact to encourage new growth.

How to Harvest

  • Cut from the outside: Harvest leaves from the outside of the clump, cutting them about 2‚Äì3cm (1in) above the base. This encourages new growth from the centre.
  • Harvest regularly: Regular harvesting encourages more vigorous growth and prevents plants from becoming overcrowded. Cut leaves as needed throughout the growing season.
  • Use sharp scissors: Use clean, sharp scissors or a knife to cut chives. This gives a clean cut and doesn't damage the plant.
  • Harvest before flowering: Leaves have the best flavour before the plant flowers, though you can continue harvesting after flowering. Removing flower stems encourages more leaf production.

Using Your Chives

Chives are best used fresh and add a mild, delicate onion flavour to many dishes. They're perfect for adding to salads, soups, egg dishes, potatoes, fish, and many other recipes. Add chives at the end of cooking or use raw, as heat can diminish their delicate flavour. The flowers are also edible and make an attractive garnish.

Storage

Fresh chives can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days. Place stems in a glass of water like cut flowers, or wrap in damp kitchen paper and store in the fridge. Chives can be frozen, but they lose their texture and are best used in cooked dishes. They don't dry well, so fresh is always best.

Common Problems

Chives are generally trouble-free and very reliable. They're rarely affected by pests or diseases and are one of the easiest herbs to grow successfully. Most problems are minor and easily managed.

Pests

  • Onion fly: Can occasionally affect chives, causing leaves to yellow and wilt. Usually not a serious problem. Remove affected plants and practice crop rotation if growing in the ground.
  • Slugs and snails: Can damage young seedlings, but established plants are rarely affected. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls if needed.

Diseases

  • Rust: Can occasionally affect chives, causing orange spots on leaves. Usually not serious. Remove affected leaves and improve air circulation. Avoid overhead watering.
  • White rot: A fungal disease that can affect alliums. If it occurs, remove affected plants and don't replant chives or other alliums in the same spot for several years.

Environmental Issues

  • Overcrowding: Chives can become overcrowded after a few years, leading to reduced growth. Divide plants every 3‚Äì4 years to maintain vigour.
  • Poor growth: Can occur if plants don't receive enough water or are growing in very poor soil. Ensure adequate watering and improve soil with compost if needed.
  • Flowering too early: If chives flower very early, it may indicate stress. Ensure plants have adequate water and nutrients. Remove flower stems to encourage leaf production.
