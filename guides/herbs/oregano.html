Complete Guide to Growing Fresh Oregano in the UK

Getting Started

Oregano (Origanum vulgare) is a hardy perennial herb with a strong, pungent flavour that's essential in Mediterranean cuisine. This aromatic herb is easy to grow in UK gardens and will return year after year, making it a valuable addition to any herb garden. Oregano is closely related to marjoram but has a stronger, more robust flavour.

Key Characteristics:

  • Hardy perennial that returns year after year
  • Grows 30‚Äì60cm (1‚Äì2ft) tall with spreading habit
  • Produces small pink or white flowers in summer
  • Strong, pungent flavour
  • Essential in Mediterranean cooking
  • Very easy to grow and low maintenance

Oregano is native to the Mediterranean region and has been cultivated for centuries for its culinary and medicinal uses. The leaves have a strong, pungent flavour that's more intense than marjoram, making it perfect for pizza, pasta, and other Italian and Greek dishes. Oregano is often confused with marjoram, but it has a stronger, more robust flavour.

In the UK, oregano is fully hardy and will return year after year with minimal care. It's a very easy herb to grow and requires little maintenance once established. The plant will spread gradually, but it's not as aggressive as mint, so it can be grown in the ground with proper spacing.

Choosing Varieties

There are several varieties of oregano available, each with slightly different flavours and characteristics. The most common is wild oregano (Origanum vulgare), but there are also some ornamental and culinary varieties worth exploring.

Oregano is typically grown from seeds, cuttings, or division. When purchasing oregano, you're usually buying seeds or small plants. The main consideration is whether you want the standard wild oregano or one of the more ornamental varieties, which can add visual interest to the garden.

Recommended Varieties

Wild Oregano

The standard variety (Origanum vulgare). Most widely available and reliable performer. Has the characteristic strong, pungent flavour. Best choice for UK gardens and culinary use.

Greek Oregano

Particularly strong-flavoured variety (Origanum vulgare subsp. hirtum). Excellent for cooking with intense flavour. Slightly more tender than wild oregano but still hardy in UK.

Golden Oregano

Ornamental variety with golden-yellow leaves (Origanum vulgare 'Aureum'). Less vigorous than green oregano but adds visual interest. Same culinary uses.

Compact Oregano

Dwarf variety that's perfect for containers (Origanum vulgare 'Compactum'). Same flavour as wild oregano but more compact growth. Ideal for small spaces.

Sowing Oregano

Sowing Outdoors

Oregano can be sown directly outdoors from mid-spring to early summer. It's very easy to grow from seed and will germinate reliably. Sow seeds in their final position or in a seedbed for transplanting later.

  1. Prepare the site: Choose a sunny location with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. Remove weeds and rake the soil to a fine tilth.
  2. Sow seeds: Sow seeds thinly on the soil surface or cover very lightly (seeds need light to germinate). Space rows 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart.
  3. Water gently: Water the soil gently after sowing, taking care not to wash away the seeds. Keep the soil moist until germination.
  4. Thin seedlings: When seedlings are large enough to handle, thin them to 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart. Oregano spreads gradually, so give it plenty of space.
  5. Transplant if needed: If you've sown in a seedbed, transplant seedlings to their final position when they're large enough to handle.

Sowing Indoors

You can start oregano indoors in late winter or early spring for an earlier start. Sow seeds in pots or seed trays filled with peat-free seed compost. Cover seeds very lightly or not at all, as they need light to germinate. Keep at 15‚Äì20¬∞C (59‚Äì68¬∞F) and maintain consistent moisture. Seeds should germinate within 10‚Äì14 days. Transplant seedlings outdoors after the last frost, hardening them off first.

üí° Sowing Tip: Oregano seeds are very small and need light to germinate, so don't cover them with too much soil. A very light covering or no covering at all is best. The seeds will germinate reliably if kept moist and at the right temperature. Once established, oregano will return year after year.

Planting

Oregano prefers a sunny position with well-drained soil. It can be grown in containers or in the ground, and it's fully hardy in the UK. The key is to provide good drainage and plenty of sunlight.

Site Selection

Choose a sunny location with well-drained, reasonably fertile soil. Oregano prefers full sun but will tolerate light shade. It will not grow well in heavy, waterlogged soil, so ensure good drainage. Avoid very dry, poor soil, as this will result in weak growth. Oregano grows best in soil that's not too rich, as overly fertile soil can encourage excessive growth at the expense of flavour.

Container Planting

Growing oregano in containers is an excellent option, especially for compact varieties. Use a container at least 20cm (8in) wide and deep with good drainage. Fill with peat-free multi-purpose compost. Container-grown oregano will need more frequent watering than ground-grown plants. You can also grow different varieties in separate containers to enjoy a range of flavours.

Ground Planting

If planting in the ground, prepare the soil by removing weeds and incorporating well-rotted compost if the soil is very poor. However, avoid over-enriching the soil. Space plants 30‚Äì45cm (12‚Äì18in) apart to allow for spreading. Oregano spreads gradually but is not as aggressive as mint, so it can be grown in the ground with proper spacing.

üí° Planting Tip: Oregano is fully hardy in the UK and will return year after year. It's a great choice for a low-maintenance herb garden. The plant spreads gradually, so give it plenty of space, but it's not as invasive as mint, so it can be grown in the ground without barriers.

Plant Care

Oregano is very low-maintenance once established. It's a hardy perennial that will return year after year with minimal care. However, a few simple practices will help ensure healthy growth and good harvests.

Watering

Water oregano regularly during dry spells, especially when plants are young. Once established, oregano is quite drought-tolerant, but regular watering will encourage more vigorous growth and better leaf quality. Container-grown plants will need more frequent watering than those in the ground. Water at the base of plants to avoid wetting the leaves, which can lead to fungal issues.

Feeding

Oregano doesn't usually need additional feeding if grown in reasonably fertile soil. In fact, overly rich soil can encourage excessive growth at the expense of flavour. However, if your soil is very poor, you can apply a balanced, organic fertiliser in spring. Container-grown plants may benefit from a light liquid feed every 4‚Äì6 weeks during the growing season. Avoid over-fertilising, as this can reduce the intensity of the flavour.

Pruning and Cutting Back

Regular harvesting of leaves helps keep oregano bushy and encourages new growth. Cut back plants by about one-third after flowering to encourage fresh growth. You can also cut back hard in early spring to remove old growth and encourage new shoots. Regular harvesting also helps prevent plants from becoming leggy.

Dividing

Oregano benefits from being divided every 3‚Äì4 years to maintain vigour. Divide plants in spring or autumn by digging up the clump (or removing from container) and separating it into smaller sections. Replant the divisions immediately in fresh compost. This is also a great way to propagate new plants and share with friends.

Winter Care

Oregano is fully hardy and will die back in winter, then regrow in spring. In autumn, cut back any dead foliage. The roots will survive underground and send up new growth in spring. Container-grown plants may benefit from some protection in very cold areas‚Äîmove containers to a sheltered spot or wrap them in fleece. In mild winters, oregano may retain some leaves.

Harvesting

Oregano leaves can be harvested as soon as plants are large enough, usually 6‚Äì8 weeks after planting. The leaves are best used fresh, but they can also be dried for later use. Dried oregano retains its flavour well, making it a popular herb for storage. Harvest regularly to encourage new growth and keep plants bushy.

How to Harvest

  • Pick young leaves: Harvest leaves when they're young and tender for the best flavour. Older leaves can become tough and less flavourful.
  • Harvest regularly: Pick leaves regularly throughout the growing season to encourage new growth and prevent plants from becoming leggy.
  • Cut whole stems: You can cut whole stems near the base, or pick individual leaves as needed. Cutting stems encourages bushier growth.
  • Harvest before flowering: For the best flavour, harvest leaves before the plant flowers. However, the flowers are attractive to bees, so you may want to allow some to flower.

Using Your Oregano

Fresh oregano leaves add a strong, pungent flavour to many dishes. Use them in pizza, pasta, Mediterranean cuisine, and meat dishes. They're excellent with tomatoes, lamb, vegetables, and in sauces. Oregano has a stronger flavour than marjoram, so use it when you want a more robust herb flavour. Add oregano at the beginning of cooking to allow the flavour to develop, or use fresh as a garnish.

Drying Oregano

Oregano is one of the best herbs for drying, as it retains its flavour well. Harvest leaves in the morning after the dew has dried. Dry them in a warm, well-ventilated area out of direct sunlight, or use a dehydrator. Store dried leaves in an airtight container in a cool, dark place. Dried oregano retains its flavour better than many other herbs and can be stored for up to a year.

Freezing Oregano

Oregano can be frozen for use in cooked dishes. Freeze whole leaves on a tray, then transfer to freezer bags, or chop leaves and freeze in ice cube trays with a little water. Frozen oregano is best used in cooked dishes rather than raw applications, as freezing can affect the texture.

Storage

Fresh oregano leaves can be stored in the refrigerator for a few days. Place stems in a glass of water like cut flowers, or wrap in damp kitchen paper and store in the fridge. For longer storage, dry the leaves as described above. Dried oregano can be stored in an airtight container for up to a year, and it retains its flavour better than many other dried herbs.

Common Problems

Oregano is generally very trouble-free and rarely suffers from serious pests or diseases. However, there are a few issues to watch out for, mostly related to moisture and soil conditions.

Pests

  • Aphids: Can cluster on new growth, especially in spring. Usually not serious and can be controlled by encouraging natural predators or using organic methods.
  • Spider mites: Can occur in hot, dry conditions. Increase humidity and improve air circulation. Usually not serious if caught early.
  • Slugs and snails: Can damage young leaves, especially in spring. Use barriers, traps, or organic controls. Once established, oregano is less attractive to slugs.

Diseases

  • Root rot: Can occur if plants are overwatered or if drainage is poor. Ensure good drainage and avoid overwatering. Container-grown plants are particularly susceptible if drainage is poor.
  • Powdery mildew: Can occur in humid conditions or when plants are overcrowded. Improve air circulation and avoid overhead watering. Usually not serious if caught early.

Environmental Issues

  • Poor flavour: Can occur if plants are over-fertilised or grown in overly rich soil. Oregano grows best in soil that's not too fertile, as this helps develop the characteristic strong flavour.
  • Leggy growth: Can occur if plants aren't pruned regularly. Cut back plants by about one-third after flowering and harvest leaves regularly to encourage bushy growth.
  • Insufficient light: Can cause weak, leggy growth. Ensure plants receive bright light‚Äîfull sun is best for oregano.
  • Drying out: Oregano needs consistent moisture and will suffer if allowed to dry out completely. Water regularly, especially container-grown plants.

üí° Growing Tip: Oregano is one of the easiest herbs to grow and is perfect for beginners. It's very forgiving and will thrive with minimal care. The key to success is providing good drainage, plenty of sunlight, and avoiding over-fertilising. With proper care, you'll have a steady supply of fresh, pungent oregano for your cooking, and it will return year after year!
